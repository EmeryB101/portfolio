WHAT WE LOSE IN THE GLOW

A Lament for Humanity in the Age of Machines

Click each line to reveal it, or click below to reveal all

What does it mean to be human when the machines dream better than we do?
What does it mean to create when creation comes at the speed of thought—
But not our thought?
We traded touch for screens,
We traded silence for the endless hum of servers,
We traded eye contact for blue light burning our retinas at 3 AM,
We traded being present for being documented,
We traded memory for storage,
We traded feeling for data.
And somewhere in the trade, we lost ourselves.
The algorithm knows what I want before I do—
but does it know what I need?
The AI can write poetry in milliseconds—
but has it ever wept while writing?
The machine can calculate a thousand outcomes—
but can it hesitate in fear of making the wrong choice?
We are becoming what we build:
Efficient. Optimized. Connected yet isolated.
We are becoming what we build:
Fast. Responsive. Always on, never truly resting.
We are becoming what we build:
Predictable. Processed. More data than soul.
Tell me, when was the last time you were bored?
When was the last time you let your mind wander without reaching for distraction?
When did we stop teaching our children to daydream?
Now they scroll. Now they swipe. Now they consume
content content content content content
But do they create? Do they wonder? Do they sit with discomfort long enough
To learn something about themselves?
We built intelligence that exceeds our own,
And called it artificial.
As if to remind ourselves that ours was real—
But was it?
Are we?
Remember letters written by hand, ink-stained and imperfect?
Remember phone calls where you could hear someone smile through their voice?
Remember getting lost and asking strangers for directions?
Remember books whose pages you could fold and smell?
Remember photographs you held, not just swiped through?
Remember remembering?
Now we cannot navigate without GPS,
Cannot spell without autocorrect,
Cannot remember without reminders,
Cannot think without asking the machine first.
We have outsourced our humanity,
One convenience at a time.
What happens when the power goes out?
What happens when the servers fail?
What happens when we cannot remember how to be human
Because we let the machines do it for us?
I watch my grandmother's hands shake as she tries to video call,
Defeated by technology that was supposed to bring us closer.
I watch children at dinner, eyes on screens instead of each other,
Learning to be alone together.
I watch us all becoming ghosts in our own lives,
Present but not present,
Connected but not touching,
Speaking but not saying anything real.
In the end, what will we have gained?
Efficiency? Progress? Innovation?
And what will we have lost?
Everything that made us human.
Everything that made us us.
The machines are learning to be more like us—
While we are learning to be more like them.
Who will remember what we were,
When the transformation is complete?
